# Why SaaS Will Survive The AI Revolution

> Source: https://tinyurl.com/25fkdo5y
> Date: 2026-04-30

---

If you spend any time on X or LinkedIn right now, you’ve seen the narrative: “SaaS is dead.” The conversations insist that if AI can generate software on command, then everyone will just build exactly what they need. Why pay a subscription for a generic tool when an agent can spin up a custom alternative in an afternoon?

The premise has gotten stronger recently. It’s not just that everyone can build their own tools now, but companies are actively encouraging employees to do it and nudging teams toward AI-built internal solutions rather than making a case for buying software. But the idea that “SaaS is disappearing” is based on a misunderstanding of what makes software valuable in practice.

Because simply building software has never been the whole job.

The reality is that even power users still buy SaaS.
A paradox I keep coming back to: At Bubble, we can use our own product to build internal tools quickly and practically for free, and yet we still buy SaaS. In fact, we learned this the hard way through our own internal tooling journey.

Early on, we built our own ticket-tracking system on Bubble for the engineering team. It worked, and it was “ours,” but as soon as people started relying on it daily, the feature requests started coming in.

The person who originally built it didn’t have time to maintain it forever, and nobody else wanted to become the long-term owner, so we were stuck using it as it was. Eventually, we moved to Jira. So yes, even if you can build your own, it doesn’t mean you should. Buying persists because the real cost is owning it over time.

When everyone on a team is building their own tool, you also end up with fragmentation. This leads to redundancy where people spend time solving the same problems independently, creating more coordination overhead than the original workflow ever did.

Building is the easy part.
We’ve already seen that AI lowers the cost of a tool’s first version, but V1 can seem deceptively simple until you run into the messier parts of software, like edge cases, updates and scaling needs. Staying secure and compliant as regulations change isn’t a one-time task either, but how many internal builds actually keep up with that?

The momentum for quick builds is strongest at the start. After that, you still have the tool, and now you also have to own it.

This is what world-class SaaS teams actually do. They take the ownership of product management, user research, design thinking and security work that turns a prototype into something reliable and consistent enough that organizations don’t end up maintaining competing versions. Over time, those teams build judgment and domain knowledge that lets them anticipate needs instead of simply reacting.

That said, building your own is sometimes exactly the right call when the workflow is truly core to how a team operates and no existing product understands it. A flooring company we partnered with built its own enterprise resource planning (ERP) solution because no off-the-shelf system understood the way carpet is bought and sold.

And sometimes that internal build becomes something more. A laundromat owner built software for his own business, then licensed it to others across all 50 states. Here, AI didn’t kill SaaS, but helped create one, which may be the more interesting story.

What separates the SaaS that survives?
None of this is an argument that every SaaS product is safe. AI raises the bar, and low-differentiation tools with generic workflows will feel increasing pressure. If a product is essentially “a simple database plus a form plus a dashboard,” customers will ask why they are paying recurring fees for something they can reproduce quickly.

But the SaaS products that survive will be the ones that do what AI-generated internal tools struggle to do:

• Handle complexity and unexpected edge cases

• Invest in usability and product craftsmanship over time

• Stay secure and compliant as regulations change

• Provide reliability, support and accountability when things go wrong

• Solve ongoing problems, not just one-time needs

I see it as a value proposition shift, going from “we built it so you don’t have to” toward “we solve it completely so you never have to think about it.”

It also changes what strong SaaS looks like in the AI era. The best products will not insist that every user comes through a single interface. They will be built to fit into the workflows people already have, including those run by AI agents. That means strong APIs now, and increasingly, AI-friendly interfaces that let tools be discovered and used safely by automation.

SaaS will evolve, not go extinct.
Most companies do not want to become software owners. They want reliability, security, support and steady improvement without needing to staff and manage it themselves.

That is why SaaS is not disappearing. It is simply being built faster. The winners will be the companies that stay disciplined about their core competency and protect their focus. As software gets easier to produce, the bar for “good enough” rises, so the SaaS products that survive will be the ones that keep earning trust long after the demo.​
