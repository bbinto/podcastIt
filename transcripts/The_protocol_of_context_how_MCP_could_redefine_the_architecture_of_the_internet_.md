The protocol of context: how MCP could redefine the architecture of the internet

So far, the AI revolution has been characterized by models that generate text, code or images that imitate human creativity. But the real promise, which will define how we reorganize our relationship with the internet, is about more than the quality of the answers, and in how these models connect to the real world: the tools, data and services we use every day. The Model Context Protocol (MCP) developed by Anthropic and now in the hands of the Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation, aims to extablish the principles for the next architecture of the internet.

MCP is an open standard that defines how large language models and AI agents discover, authenticate, and use external tools, data sources and resources available in connected services. If until now “isolated” AI is based on static knowledge or ad hoc integrations, MCP proposes a common language for agents to interact dynamically and in real time with the external elements they need to complete tasks. A universal connector that some have dubbed a USB-C for AI, it dramatically reduces the complexity of integration.

This type of protocol has profound social and economic implications. OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, AWS, Block and Cloudflare see it as the future because they believe that models should no longer be merely receivers of instructions, but active agents capable of executing complex tasks on behalf of the user. Instead of opening a website, searching, copying and pasting or filling out forms, imagine delegating those actions to an AI that negotiates, plans or buys for us, accessing the services we need with secure permissions. This transition from the traditional graphical interface to agents operating through standards such as CCM is perhaps the most radical change in decades.

But beneath the enthusiasm for standardization there are serious issues. Historically, protocols that dominate a technological era become contracts of the future: betting on HTTP and HTML5 meant committing the evolution of the web to a certain model and paradigm. Similarly, choosing MCP as the basis for a new layer of AI infrastructure implies assuming that this standard will be sufficiently broad, secure, and adaptable to diverse contexts. The history of technology is full of standards that seemed inevitable and yet were replaced (think Gopher vs. HTTP).

There are also questions about MCP’s safety and technical sustainability. Recent academic audits indicate that its flexibility makes it vulnerable to attack vectors such as executing malicious code or stealing credentials, highlighting the need for more sophisticated monitoring and defense frameworks. This underscores something essential: when we delegate autonomous action to systems that operate as agents on our behalf, we also delegate risks that are no longer just “software bugs,” but potential impacts on privacy, security, and digital trust.

The advance of CCM and the creation of the AAIF reflect a deeper tension: will there be an internet of interoperable agents, or will we see fragmentation and rivalries between closed ecosystems? Donating MCP to the Linux Foundation is about preventing a single company from controlling the standard. But even under a neutral organization, competition for tech leadership and market share can steer the evolution of these standards in directions that favor those who control the most lucrative platforms.

The vision behind it is ambitious: a network where user action is converted into intent specifications and agents execute those intentions safely and efficiently, participating in tool markets, negotiating prices, managing schedules, or even anticipating needs. This is the promise of an internet orchestrated by AI: a network not only of pages and APIs, but of agents that act as our proxies.

But that future, as always, is a contract with itself: MCP means an internet where the logic of human intermediation is diluted in autonomous protocols. The critical question is not whether these systems will be technically better, but how they will redefine our expectations of agency, accountability and control over the infrastructure that will sustain the next generation of digital services.

If this vision prospers, using the internet could be transformed into cooperating with agents, a process already underway at other levels. And this alliance between humans and machines will not only be a technological advance, but a renegotiation of the terms under which we delegate our actions to systems that now speak the same language: the protocol of context.
