I stumbled into a bookstore and a book caught my eye:
A More Beautiful Question by Warren Berger.

I thought it’d be a quick, casual read.

Instead, it ended up changing the way I think, lead, and make decisions.

It also reminded me how bad we’ve become at asking good questions.

And here’s what surprised me most: the difference between people who stall out at Senior IC and those who break into Director or VP roles often comes down to one thing. Not technical skill. Not even executive presence.

It’s the questions they ask.

The Question Deficit We Don’t Talk About
Did you know children ask 40,000 questions by age 5?

Then it drops drastically.

Think about it.

How many questions did you really ask in your last three meetings? How many times did you pause to interrogate an assumption before building a roadmap? How many strategies did you accept without asking “why this, why now?”

If your answer to all three questions is “not many”, it’s not your fault.

We’ve been primed to:

Prioritize fixing over asking

Value answers over curiosity

Avoid looking stupid by not knowing

This is a major career limiter.

I learned this the hard way three years ago when I pitched a major program to leadership. I had the timeline, the budget, and the resource plan all buttoned up.

Then, my VP asked one question: “What problem are we actually solving?”

I froze.

I had answers to some questions, but I hadn’t asked the most important questions first.

That program never launched.

And I realized something: I’d been so focused on having answers that I forgot to interrogate the problem itself.

Here’s what the data shows. Research from Harvard Business School found that people who ask more questions, especially follow-up questions, are better liked and learn more information from their conversation partners. But in professional settings, we ask fewer questions as we gain seniority because we think we’re supposed to have the answers.

That’s backwards.

The best leaders I’ve worked with, the ones running billion-dollar companies, ask relentless questions. They do this not to test you, but to understand the problem deeply before they act.

Actionable Advice:
Track how many questions you ask in your next three meetings. Be honest about the number.

Notice when you jump to solutions. Pause and ask one clarifying question first.

Practice saying “I don’t know yet, but here’s what I’m asking” instead of defaulting to a half-baked answer.

Good Questions Spark Clarity
This sounds simple, but it’s true.

A good question forces us to pause, reflect, and readjust. That’s how you build with intention and focus on quality over speed.

Let me give you an example.

Last year, I was working with a Senior PM who kept getting feedback that she needed to “think more strategically” to get promoted.

This frustrated her.

She was already working 60-hour weeks.

What more did they want?

I asked her: “What does strategic actually mean to your leadership?”

She paused. “I’m not sure.”

So we reframed.

Instead of working harder, we asked better questions:
What decisions do Directors make that I don’t?

What information do they have access to that I don’t?

What problems are they solving that I’m not seeing yet?

Within two months, she started asking those questions in her 1:1s.

She stopped waiting for her manager to tell her what strategic meant.

Instead, she asked him directly.

She also asked peer Directors.

Asked her skip-level.

Six months later, she got promoted.

Not because she worked harder, but because she asked the questions that clarified the path.

Here’s what I’ve noticed across hundreds of career conversations: the people who advance aren’t the ones with perfect answers; they’re the ones who ask questions that reframe the problem entirely.

They don’t ask “How do I get promoted?” They ask, “What does my leadership need from this role that they’re not getting yet?”

They don’t ask “Why didn’t I get the VP role?” They ask, “What capabilities does the person who got it have that I need to build?”

See the difference?

One type of question keeps you stuck in reaction mode.

The other gives you agency.

Actionable Advice:
Write down the problem you’re facing right now in your career. Now ask: “What’s the question I’m not asking about this?”

Before your next 1:1, write three questions you want answers to. Make one of them about something you don’t understand yet.

When you get vague feedback, ask “Can you give me an example of what that looks like?” Then ask “What would great look like?”

A Simple But Good Framework: Why → What If → How
I loved this framework from A More Beautiful Question because it’s stupidly simple.

But it works.

Why? (Why is this the way it is?)
What if? (What if there's another way?)
How? (How might I bring it to life?)

Let’s say someone says, “I need a better job.”

Instead of jumping into action mode, try this:

Why?
Why does my current job feel misaligned?

This helps surface what’s actually not working.

Is it burnout?

Is it a values mismatch?

Do I actually want growth, not just a promotion?

Am I running from something or running toward something?

I had a client who was convinced she needed to leave her company. We spent 30 minutes just on “why.”

Turns out, she didn’t hate her company.

She hated that her manager kept her on maintenance work instead of high-impact projects.

That’s a very different problem.

And it has a very different solution.

What if?
What if I could redesign my role before quitting?

This is where possibility lives.

What if I could get staffed on a strategic initiative?

What if I could propose a new program that solves a gap I see?

What if I could negotiate for more interesting projects instead of a promotion?

My client asked her manager: “What if I took on the Q3 platform migration project?”

Her manager said yes.

Three months later, she was leading a cross-functional team of 12 people.

Six months later, she got promoted.

She didn’t need a new job.

She needed to ask a different question.

How?
How might I explore new opportunities without jumping ship?

This is where action meets reality.

How do I build a relationship with the team that owns strategic work?

How do I signal to my manager that I’m ready for more scope?

How do I demonstrate the skills I need for the next level?

One reframe turns into three powerful, actionable reflections.

Actionable Advice:
Take one decision you’re facing right now. Write it down.

Ask “Why” three times. Don’t skip this. Get to the root.

Ask “What if” for every assumption you’ve been holding. Write down at least three alternatives.

Pick one “How” and do it this week. Make it small. Make it real.
