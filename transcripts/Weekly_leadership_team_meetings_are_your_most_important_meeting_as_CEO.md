Your weekly leadership team meeting is broken. Here’s how to fix it.
How to eliminate the hub-and-spoke model and create real alignment
DAVID POLITIS
APR 23, 2026

Most companies already have leadership meetings. They’re just not effective. Here’s what I’ve seen after operating three companies and advising dozens of CEOs.

Communication across the leadership team happens through email, Slack, one-on-ones, short stand-ups a few times a week, and a longer weekly meeting that nobody wants to be in. The weekly meeting runs for an hour, gets canceled when people travel, and they always run out of time before getting to anything important.

Based on my experience, I believe 75% of leadership teams are not working in lockstep. They’re disconnected, working in silos, not aligned on priorities. They’re not accountable to each other, and they’re using you in a hub-and-spoke model.

I spent years running my first company like this. My CRO and I would discuss pipeline strategy in a one-on-one. Then I’d relay it to my CMO during our one-on-one (although some details got lost in translation). When those two met to coordinate, they were working off different versions of the same conversation.

Your company is only as effective as your leadership team. That’s why I push CEOs to invest the time, energy, and resources into a strong weekly leadership team meeting.

The leadership meeting you need
When I say “leadership team,” I’m talking about your functional leaders (the people running each core function of your business). Early on, that might be managers or team leads, or maybe even individual contributors. Over time, it transitions to VPs and the C-suite. You’ll often have a mix of leadership levels in the room.

Title doesn’t matter. You need functional representation and an effective agenda.

But not the boring status update meeting where everyone reads numbers out loud. And not the meeting where people discuss a couple of projects and decide to schedule additional meetings to discuss them further.

I’m talking about a real leadership meeting where every functional leader in your company sits in the same room, hears the same information, debates the same decisions, and leaves aligned.

This is your most important meeting as CEO. It’s where you eliminate the hub-and-spoke chaos, and you tear down silos, where strategic initiatives get pressure-tested before they go to the company or the board, and where your leadership team actually becomes a team instead of a group of individuals reporting to you.

Why this is your most important meeting
Many leaders think their most critical meetings are the one-on-ones with each functional leader. These are important, but leadership team meetings carry significant weight. They connect cross-functional leaders, drive alignment, and save you time being the messenger.

It’s where alignment actually happens
When your CRO, CMO, CTO, or any functional leader at the time are all in the room hearing the same information at the same time, they’re less likely to walk away with different interpretations. When your head of customer success hears directly from the product team about what’s coming, why they prioritized it, and has a chance to ask questions, they can plan accordingly and empower their team to communicate more effectively with customers.

When everyone sees the same priorities, the same trade-offs, and the same constraints and risks, you don’t have to spend the next week realigning people who were never aligned in the first place.

It turns your leadership team into an actual team
When your CTO presents a major technology infrastructure project to the full leadership team, they have to explain why it matters to the business, not just to the engineering team. When your head of sales asks for more headcount, they have to make that case in front of the CFO and discuss the trade-offs. This is how leaders learn to think beyond their function.

It’s where strategic work gets pressure-tested first
Every major initiative at your company should be discussed here first. Fundraising, M&A, the customer conference, and major product launches. If it matters to the company, it should get pressure-tested in this room first before it goes to the company all-hands or the board meeting. You get questions, you get pushback, and you get better ideas.

Then, by the time you do present to the company or the board, it’s been refined by the key leaders in your organization. You get all the context and all the decision-makers in the same room at once. That’s what makes the weekly leadership meeting irreplaceable.

Setting up your leadership team meeting
Here’s what you need to know before you schedule your first leadership team meeting.

When to start and who attends
Start this meeting once your company has leaders for each function, or most functions. That could be when the company is 25-30 people. Before that, you probably don’t need the formality of leadership meetings. In the real early days, the company is still small enough that a weekly all-hands meeting drives the alignment you need.

Title doesn’t matter, functional representation matters. Every person who is leading a function at your company should attend. If you have a customer success lead and no one above them, they should be in the room.

This group will change over time. You might have your director of sales in the room today, then hire a CRO who replaces them. That’s ok, and expected, just set this expectation upfront.

Logistics
Make this weekly and non-negotiable. This meeting shouldn’t get booked over. It doesn’t get canceled. If people are traveling or on PTO, they should be expected to read the meeting notes and/or watch the recording. That said, I recommend avoiding travel to accommodate the meeting. It’s that critical.

I suggest scheduling these Tuesdays at a time that works best for you and your team. Avoid Mondays. Too many long weekends and vacations that bleed into Monday. Let people catch up and start their week. You’ll also be asking them to submit their agenda items and updates in advance so attendees have time to review everything (ideally 24 hours before the meeting). Depending on company size, these meetings might take 90 minutes to 3 hours.

I recently spoke with a CEO who wanted to cap their weekly leadership team meeting at 60 minutes. The problem is that the entire meeting was people saying, “We’ll take that offline.” You need time for real conversations. I suggested he lengthen them, and he couldn’t believe how much more valuable they became just by giving the topics space to breathe.

Rules of engagement
Set these expectations explicitly:

Everything discussed here is confidential unless decided otherwise.

This isn’t a democracy; you want input, but you (the CEO) should make the final decisions.

Everyone must speak up - create a safe space where people aren’t afraid to say what’s on their mind.

Anyone with follow-up items is responsible for actually completing them.

No use of phones or computers unless you’re using them for the meeting or there’s an emergency.

The people in this room may change over time. The format and length may also change.

The goal is alignment and accountability.

Part 1: Recurring items (30-45 minutes)
This section stays the same every week. Start by reviewing the top KPIs for the business. Then, have each functional leader share the following:

Highlights and headwinds
Ask your functional leaders to submit three highlights and three headwinds in the agenda doc, and have them come prepared to discuss one of each. Prior to the meeting, each leader should fill out their section of the agenda. This gives them time to think through what actually matters instead of making things up on the spot.

We used to do this verbally in the meeting, but people weren’t prepared. They rambled, and it took forever. Once we moved to written updates in advance, everything changed. People came prepared, they were concise, and we actually had time to discuss what mattered.

Priority changes
This is the “change order” section of the agenda, and it’s critical. When a big opportunity comes in, you can’t just decide to do it and expect everyone to get on board and absorb it. Think of it like a contractor working on your house. They can’t just show up and charge you extra. They have to have a conversation: “This wasn’t in the original plan, here’s the impact, here’s what it will cost, and here’s the tradeoff”.

Without this section, alignment gets destroyed because, in reality, things will change, and your leadership team needs to know about those changes first; otherwise, it all unravels. Prioritizing changes as a team forces everyone to see the trade-offs in real time.

Key meetings and milestones
This is a quick two-minute update on what’s happening this week. The upcoming board meeting, a flagship customer visiting the office, and the upcoming holiday that requires rescheduling the all-hands. You need to make sure everyone knows what’s on the calendar that affects them and their teams.

Part 2: Strategic topics (60-90 minutes)
This is the bulk of the leadership meeting, and that’s why it’s so valuable. Most leadership meetings only cover the recurring stuff, KPIs, and general updates. That’s not deep enough. The strategic topics section is where you actually get work done as a leadership team.

You’ll have 1-3 strategic topics each week based on what’s happening in the business. Different leaders present their topics (it’s not just you talking for 90 minutes). Each functional leader should come prepared to discuss their topic and share slides, reports, mockups, etc., with the team in advance so everyone has time to review and prepare questions.

When it makes sense, I love to bring in people who aren’t on the leadership team to present. Maybe your customer support manager presents CSAT improvements. Maybe your sales engineer presents on a technical POC. This is huge for development.

An important note: You’ll quickly build a backlog of topics. Don’t squeeze them all in. Give each subject the time it needs and keep reprioritizing. You’ll also have some regular items: board meeting prep, quarterly results review, and product roadmap updates.

Strategic topics typically fall into three categories:

Telling and answering questions
You’re not asking for permission or trying to change the plan. You’re informing the team and taking questions.

Example: Your board meeting is in three weeks. You walk the team through the agenda, the slides you need from each person, and the timeline. You’re not debating whether to have a board meeting; you’re making sure everyone knows what’s expected and can ask questions about their part.

Rough draft and feedback
You have a plan, but you want input to make it better.

Example: You’re implementing a new performance review process. You already know you’re doing reviews, you already know when, and you generally know the format. But you want the leadership team’s input on the specific questions to ask, how many people should provide feedback, etc. You’re refining the plan, not starting from scratch.

Brainstorming
You’re genuinely exploring options and want the team to help you think through it.

Example: You’re considering growing through acquisition. You want to discuss which category would move the needle the most for the company. Or you’re redefining company values and want the team to brainstorm what matters most. These are open-ended discussions where you’re building the plan together.

Part 3: Follow-ups from last meeting (5-15 minutes)
This part is simple, and it’s all about accountability: What did we say we were going to do last week, and did we do it? If not, why?

Review the action items from the previous meeting and add new items to the follow-up list based on what came up in today’s strategic topics. If you have an EA or chief of staff, they might track the follow-up items. But the responsibility to deliver is on the person who committed to it. You shouldn’t have to remind people. They should come to the meeting ready to report on what they said they’d do.

Part 4: The elephant in the room (15-30 minutes)
End every leadership meeting with this question: “What’s the elephant in the room we’re not saying out loud?”

Most people treat leadership meetings like a routine. They agree because they don’t want to create conflict. They want to be easy to work with, and they worry they’re the only ones thinking something, so they stay quiet. Then, everyone walks out of the meeting, and the real conversation happens in the hallway or over Slack.

This section forces the conversation into the room.

Examples:

“You said that feature was coming out a week late, but it’s now three months late. What’s different this time?”

“We’re saying we’re going to hit that sales number, but the pipeline doesn’t look like it’s possible. Do we need to take a different approach?”

“We just spent an hour talking about the customer conference, but I don’t think anyone actually believes we can pull this off with the current budget. Should we re-evaluate?”

This is where people say what they’re actually thinking instead of what they think they’re supposed to say.

The companies that do this well tell me it’s the most important part of the meeting. It creates space for honest conversations. It catches problems before they become disasters. It builds trust because people see that you actually want to hear what they’re thinking, not just what sounds good.

If you skip this section, people will walk out of your leadership meeting thinking one thing and saying another.

How this connects to your operating rhythm
The materials you create for your leadership team meeting become the materials for everything else.

When you discuss a strategic topic in your leadership meeting (customer conference planning, new product launch, company values, or a major partnership), you’re creating a deck or document to present it. That same deck or document (refined after your leadership team asks questions and pokes holes in it) becomes the content for your all-hands meeting, and a subset of it becomes content for your board meeting.

I used to spend three days putting together my board deck. Another CEO who was further ahead in his journey asked me, “Why don’t you just use the decks from your leadership team meeting?” I said, “What decks?” He said, “Don’t you have strategic topics you discuss?” I did, but I wasn’t thinking about it this way.

Once I started doing this, it saved me a lot of time and drove more alignment across the organization. If something was important enough to put in the board deck, we discussed it in the leadership meeting first. The team asked questions, challenged assumptions, and ultimately made it better. Then we presented it to the company at the all-hands meeting and finally, to the board.

Everything flows from this meeting
If reducing COGS is your company’s top priority right now, your CTO should discuss it in the leadership team meeting. If the most important thing is hitting your sales number, but the pipeline doesn’t support it, that should be a strategic topic. If you’re about to launch a major initiative, your leadership team should see it before anyone else.

This is how everything connects. When you have a real operating rhythm, these things aren’t separate; they’re the same pieces of information flowing through different groups of people.

The leadership meeting is where it all starts.

Key takeaways
Your weekly leadership team meeting is more important than your one-on-ones, all-hands, and board meetings because it’s the only place where all your functional leaders hear the same information at the same time and can actually create alignment.

Start this meeting when your company reaches 25-30 people. Every person leading a function should be in the room. Title doesn’t matter, functional representation does.

Use this four-part agenda: recurring items, strategic topics, follow-ups, and the elephant in the room.

The materials you create for this meeting can become content for your all-hands meeting and board deck slides. Discuss important topics with your leadership team first, then present refined versions to the company and board.
